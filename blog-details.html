Understanding Spectral Editing

Published on May 5, 2026 by Studio Head

Spectral editing is often described as "Photoshop for audio." Traditional audio editing works in the time domain, where you see a waveform that represents volume over time. Spectral editing adds a third dimension: frequency.

Why Frequency Matters

Imagine a recording of a beautiful acoustic guitar performance, but someone coughs in the middle of a note. In a normal waveform, that cough is baked into the guitar sound. You can't just cut it out without cutting the music.

However, in the spectral domain, we can see the cough as a specific energy burst in certain frequencies. By using surgical tools, we can "erase" just those frequencies, leaving the guitar's harmonic structure intact.

"The ability to see sound as a map of frequency and time allows us to fix problems that were previously unsolvable."

Common Use Cases

  • Removing sirens or birds from film dialogue.
  • Isolating individual instruments for remixing.
  • Removing electrical hum without losing bass frequencies.
